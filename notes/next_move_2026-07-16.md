### Dissemination constraint

Brett cannot deposit mathematics papers on arXiv, so the default workflow is journal-first:

1. choose the target journal and check its title-specific preprint, repository, licensing, and anonymous-review policies;
2. submit the frozen manuscript and obtain the submission acknowledgement;
3. only if the journal permits it, decide whether a public non-arXiv copy is useful.

For a public copy, Preprints.org is the closest substitute for a conventional preprint server: it accepts mathematics without endorsement, screens submissions, assigns a Crossref DOI, and indexes posted papers, but it requires CC BY 4.0 and disclosure of AI assistance. Zenodo is the more neutral archival option: anyone may deposit, it assigns a DataCite DOI, permits versioning and license choice, and is especially suitable for the verifier and certificate bundle. Do not post the same manuscript to both. If neither route adds enough value, keep the manuscript private during review and deposit the accepted manuscript later under the journal's self-archiving policy.

## Why this is the right order

The Poisson--binomial result is the only current branch with all four ingredients of a publishable theorem: a clean statement, a readable proof, two independent exact finite certificates, and a serious public-database novelty audit. Its remaining risks are identifiable interfaces and presentation, not an undefined missing idea.

The alternatives are weaker immediate bets:

- The Jerrum--Patel issue is a bounded proof-scope clarification. Their broader positive-axis obstruction survives, but the cited argument does not presently establish the exact endpoint-aligned family for every $k\geq 1$.

- Blair's 25 public witnesses add useful data but no order-at-most Pareto improvement over the existing corpus. The missing 4,420 records have archival value, not enough expected mathematical value to justify duplicate enumeration.

- The July exact searches found a strong `c-b=1` barrier but not a recursively preserved tree-DP invariant. Attacking “no cross-gap rebound” in full would currently be #993 under a new name.

## The next #993-adjacent theorem

For each fixed arm vector $\mathbf a=(a_1,\ldots,a_m)$, write

$$
F_s(x)=(1+x)^sQ(x)+xR(x),\qquad
Q=\prod_j I(P_{a_j};x),\quad
R=\prod_j I(P_{a_j-1};x).
$$

Prove a computable threshold $S(\mathbf a)$ such that $F_s$ is unimodal for every $s\geq S(\mathbf a)$. For selected hard arm vectors, check the finite range $s<S(\mathbf a)$ exactly if an all-$s$ corollary is wanted.

This target is bounded because $xR$ has fixed support: every affected coefficient increment is an explicit polynomial in $s$, while beyond that support $(1+x)^sQ$ is log-concave. It directly tests whether the Poisson--binomial reserve can control a genuine tree perturbation.

### Falsification gate

Before general proof-writing, symbolically generate all affected increments for the hardest local and Blair arm vectors, derive the proposed threshold, and replay every smaller $s$. A negative increment above the threshold refutes the bound; a finite valley refutes an all-$s$ corollary. Stop immediately if the reasoning allows $Q$ or $R$ to grow with $s$: that is the unsolved growing-broom problem, not this theorem.
