                                                                                       Introduction
                                           Every graph considered in this paper is finite and simple. For a graph G, let gi (G) denote
                                           the number of independent sets of G of cardinality i. The independence polynomial of G is
                                                                                        X
                                                                             PG (x) =      gi (G)xi .
                                                                                               i≥0

                                           Equivalently, independence polynomials are the face polynomials of independence complexes
                                           of graphs, and are therefore closely related to the study of face vectors of flag complexes.
                                           They have been studied extensively from several points of view [4, 6–8, 11] in graph theory.
                                           In 1987 [1], Alavi, Malde, Schwenk, and Erdős conjectured that the independence polyno-
                                           mials of trees are unimodal. As of April 2026, the conjecture remains open in general, and
                                           has been computationally verified for trees on at most 29 vertices [15]. Motivated by this
                                           problem, we study the existence of trees whose independence polynomials are symmetric and
                                           unimodal.
                                           A finite sequence a0 , a1 , . . . , ad of nonnegative integers is called symmetric if ai = ad−i for each
                                           0 ≤ i ≤ d. A symmetric sequence a0 , a1 , . . . , ad is called unimodal if a0 ≤ a1 ≤ · · · ≤ a⌊d/2⌋ .
                                           A polynomial P (x) = a0 +a1 x+· · ·+ad xd with ai ∈ Z≥0 is called symmetric (resp. unimodal )
                                           if its coefficient sequence is symmetric (resp. unimodal).
                                           More precisely, we address two existence questions. First, for which integers n does there exist
                                           a tree on n vertices whose independence polynomial is symmetric and unimodal? Second,
                                           for which integers d does there exist a tree whose independence polynomial is symmetric and
                                           unimodal of degree d?
                                           Our method combines two ingredients. The first is a new gluing construction for rooted
                                           graphs, introduced in the Bridge Lemma (Lemma 1.9). This lemma allows us to construct
                                           larger rooted trees from smaller ones while preserving the structure needed in our arguments.

When all γi are nonnegative, the polynomial h(x) is said to be γ-positive. This viewpoint
provides a convenient route to unimodality; see [2]. In the body of the paper, we adapt it
to rooted trees through the notion of γ-admissibility.
The notion of γ-positivity appeared first in the work of D. Foata and M. Schützenberger [9]
and it is extremely useful as it directly implies symmetry and unimodality. This application
of γ-positivity appears widely in combinatorial and geometric contexts (see [3, 5, 12, 14, 16]).
The main result of the present paper is the following.
Theorem 0.1. (1) Given n ≥ 1 with n ∈ / {2, 4, 5, 7, 10}, there is a tree on n vertices whose
independence polynomial is symmetric and unimodal.
(2) Given d ≥ 1 with d ̸= 3, there is a tree whose independence polynomial is symmetric and
unimodal of degree d.

                                        1. Bridge Lemma
In this section, we introduce a new technique to construct symmetric independence polyno-
mials of trees. Given a graph G and a vertex r ∈ V (G), the rooted graph (G, r) is the graph
G with vertex r viewed as a root. Each vertex of G can be chosen as a root.
Definition 1.1. Given two rooted graphs (G, r) and (H, s), the bridging of (G, r) and (H, s),
denoted by (G, r) ∨ (H, s), is the rooted graph with r its root which is obtained from the
disjoint union of (G, r) and (H, s) by adding the edge {r, s}.
Remark 1.2. Bridging is not necessarily associative. Let (A, r), (B, s), and (C, t) be rooted

We now introduce the notion of γ-admissibility for rooted graphs.
Definition 1.6. A rooted graph (G, r) is called γ-admissible if there exist an integer d ≥ 0
and polynomials A(y), B(y) ∈ Z≥0 [y] such that
                    PG (x) = (1 + x)d A(y),             PG−r (x) = (1 + x)d B(y).
Example 1.7. Let R3 = P3 be rooted at its middle vertex r. Then (R3 , r) is γ-admissible

The following result, which we refer to as Bridge Lemma, is useful in creating symmetric
and unimodal independence polynomials for larger graphs from smaller ones.
Lemma 1.9. (Bridge Lemma) Let (T, r) and (U, s) be γ-admissible with
                     PT (x) = (1 + x)d A(y),            PT −r (x) = (1 + x)d B(y),
                   PU (x) = (1 + x)e C(y),     PU −s (x) = (1 + x)e D(y),
where A(y), B(y), C(y), D(y) ∈ Z≥0 [y]. Suppose that A(y) − B(y) ∈ Z≥0 [y].
If (W, r) = (T, r) ∨ (U, s), then (W, r) is γ-admissible.

Proof. By the standard recursion at the root r, we have
                              PW (x) = PW −r (x) + xPW −N [r] (x).
Since W − r = (T − r) ⊔ U and W − N [r] = (T − N [r]) ⊔ (U − s), we obtain
                       PW (x) = PT −r (x)PU (x) + xPT −N [r] (x)PU −s (x).

Since xPT −N [r] (x) = PT (x) − PT −r (x), we have

                          2. Applications of the Bridge Lemma
We now apply the Bridge Lemma to rooted trees to construct a tree on n vertices with a
symmetric and unimodal independence polynomial as well as the existence of a symmetric
and unimodal independence polynomial of degree d for a tree.

Lemmas 2.1 and 2.3. Theorem 0.1 (2) follows from Lemma 2.4.

Lastly, we fix the degree of an independence polynomial and construct trees with symmetric
and unimodal independence polynomial of given degree.
Lemma 2.4. Given d ≥ 1 with d ̸= 3, there is a tree T such that PT (x) is symmetric and
unimodal with deg PT (x) = d.

Proof. We first show that degree 3 is impossible. Suppose that T is a tree on n vertices such
that PT (x) is symmetric and deg PT (x) = 3. Then
                                   PT (x) = 1 + nx + g2 x2 + x3 .
Since PT (x) is symmetric, we must have g2 = n. On the other hand, a tree on n vertices has
exactly n − 1 edges. So the number of independent sets of cardinality 2 is

that is, n2 − 5n + 2 = 0, which has no integer solution. Thus there is no tree T such that
PT (x) is symmetric and deg PT (x) = 3.
For d = 1, take T = K1 . Then PT (x) = 1 + x. Now let d ≥ 2 with d ̸= 3.
If d is even, write d = 2k with k ≥ 1. Starting from (R3 , r) and applying Lemma 1.9
using k-copies of (R3 , r), we obtain a tree whose independence polynomial is symmetric and
unimodal of degree 2k = d.
If d ∈ {5, 7, 9, 11}, one may take G8 , G11 , G14 , G17 , respectively.
Finally, let d ≥ 13 be odd. Write d = 13 + 2m with m ≥ 0. Starting from (R19 , r)
and repeatedly bridging m copies of (R3 , r) on the left, Lemma 1.9 yields a tree whose
independence polynomial is symmetric and unimodal of degree 13 + 2m = d.             □
