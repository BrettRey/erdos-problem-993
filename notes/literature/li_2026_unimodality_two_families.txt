                                          1     Introduction
                                          Let G be a simple graph with finite vertex set V (G) and edge set E(G). An independent
                                          set I (or stable set) is a subset of V (G) in which no two vertices are adjacent. The size of
                                          a largest independent set is the independence number, denoted α(G). Let ik denote the
                                          number of independent sets in G of size k, with i0 = 1 by convention. The generating
                                          function:
                                                                                           α(G)

                                                                                  IG (t) =      ik tk ,
                                                                                           k=0

                                          is called the independence polynomial of G [10].
                                              A sequence of real numbers a0 , a1 , . . . , an is said to be unimodal if there exists an index
                                          j (0 ≤ j ≤ n) such that:

                                                                  a0 ≤ a1 ≤ · · · ≤ aj−1 ≤ aj ≥ aj+1 ≥ · · · ≥ an ;

                                          it is log-concave if a2j ≥ aj−1 aj+1 for all j with 1 ≤ j ≤ n − 1. It is known that for a
                                          positive sequence, log-concavity implies unimodality. A polynomial is called unimodal (re-
                                          spectively, log-concave) if its coefficient sequence is unimodal (respectively, log-concave).

   In 1987, Alavi, Malde, Schwenk and Erdős [1] conjectured that the independence
polynomial of every tree or forest is unimodal.

Conjecture 1.1 ([1, Problem 3]). For every forest F , the independence polynomial IF (t)
is unimodal.

     This celebrated conjecture has received considerable attention. For more recent work
on this conjecture, see [2, 4, 6, 7, 11, 17, 23, 25] and references therein.
     In particular, Brown, Dilcher and Nowakowski [8] conjectured that every well-covered
graph (i.e., one in which all maximal independent sets have the same cardinality) has
a unimodal independence polynomial. Although Michael and Traves [18] later provided
counterexamples to this unimodality conjecture, it remains open for the subclass of very
well-covered graphs. Levit and Mandrescu [14] showed that for any integer α, there exists
a very well-covered tree T with α(T ) = α whose independence polynomial is log-concave.
Based on this, they conjectured that every forest has a log-concave independence poly-
nomial. This conjecture was later verified by Yosef, Mizrachi and Kadrawi [24] up to
20 vertices, and by Radcliffe (see [3]) up to 25 vertices. Using a dynamic programming
algorithm, Kadrawi, Levit, Yosef and Mizrachi [13] checked all trees up to 26 vertices
and found two trees of order 26 with non-log-concave independence polynomials, specif-

    The main result of this paper is to give the unimodality of T3,m,n and T3,m,n , thereby
providing further evidence supporting of Conjecture 1.1. To be specific, we prove the
following theorems.
Theorem 1.4. For any m, n ≥ 1, the independence polynomial of T3,m,n is unimodal.
                                                               ∗
Theorem 1.5. For any m, n ≥ 1, the independence polynomial of T3,m,n is unimodal.
    The proofs of Theorem 1.4 and Theorem 1.5 rely on the theory of chromatic sym-
metric functions. Recently, Li, Yang, Zhang and the author [16] established a connection
between the log-concavity of IG (t) and the 2-s-positivity of YG (see Section 2 for detailed
definitions.) Using this connection, they showed that the independence polynomial of any
spider graph is unimodal.
    This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we recall some necessary results related
to the chromatic symmetric functions. Section 3 introduces notations and properties that
will be frequently used through out this paper. The proof of Theorem 1.4 will be presented
in Section 4, and the proof of Theorem 1.5 is given in Section 5.

   In this section, we first recall some basic definitions of symmetric functions and then
review the essential concepts related to chromatic symmetric functions. The algebra of
symmetric functions ΛQ (x) is defined to be the subalgebra of Q[[x]] consisting of formal
power series f (x) of bounded degree and satisfying

                            f (x) = f (x1 , x2 , . . .) = f (xω(1) , xω(2) , . . .)

for every permutation ω of positive integers.
    The bases of ΛQ (x) are naturally indexed by (integer) partitions. A partition of n is
a sequence λ = (λ1 , . . . , λℓ ) such that

                    λ1 ≥ λ2 ≥ · · · ≥ λℓ > 0 and λ1 + λ2 + · · · + λℓ = n.

The number ℓ = ℓ(λ) is called the length of λ. By identifying λ = (λ1 , . . . , λℓ ) with the
infinite sequence (λ1 , . . . , λℓ , 0, 0, . . .), the Schur function sλ (x) can be defined by the dual
Jacobi-Trudi identity
                                      sλ′ (x) = det(eλi −i+j (x))1≤i,j≤ℓ ,
where eλ (x) denotes the elementary symmetric function, and λ′ is the conjugate partition
of λ, see [21] for more details. It is well known that {sλ (x)} is a base of ΛQ (x). A
symmetric function f (x) is said to be Schur-positive or simply s-positive, if it can be
expressed as a nonnegative linear combination of Schur functions. As usual, we use
[sλ ]f (x) to denote the coefficient of sλ (x) in the expansion of f (x) in terms of {sλ (x)}.
