                                                   Abstract. We study the zero sets of the independence polynomial on recursive sequences of graphs. We
                                                   prove that for a maximally independent starting graph and a stable and expanding recursion algorithm,
                                                   the zeros of the independence polynomial are uniformly bounded. Each of the recursion algorithms
                                                   leads to a rational dynamical system whose formula, degree and the dimension of the space it acts upon
                                                   depend on the specific algorithm. Nevertheless, we demonstrate that the qualitative behavior of the
                                                   dynamics exhibit universal features that can be exploited to draw conclusions about the zero sets.




                                                                                           1. Introduction
                                           1.1. Motivation and main result. In statistical physics, one typically considers the normalized limit
                                           of graph polynomials for a sequence of graphs (Gn )n≥0 that converge in an appropriate sense to an infinite
                                           limit graph. Examples include the free energy of the partition function for the Ising, hard-core, or Potts
                                           models on increasing subgraphs of a regular lattice. Besides computing the actual values of these graph
                                           polynomials, which is often infeasible for large graphs, a primary interest is a qualitative description of the
                                           limiting free energy, including a characterization of real parameters where the free energy is non-analytic,
                                           the so-called phase transitions. Obtaining such qualitative information is notoriously difficult for many
                                           graph polynomials, even for seemingly simple limit graphs such as the square lattice.
                                              There have been a number of successful studies in the last decade where the authors have considered
                                           sequences of graphs that do not converge to a regular lattice but are instead recursively defined. Notable
                                           examples of such studies include the partition function of the Ising model and the chromatic polynomial
                                           on the diamond hierarchical lattice [BLR17, BLR20, CR21], the hard-core model on Cayley trees [RL19,
                                           vW23], and the Ising model on Sierpińsky gasket graphs and Schreier graphs [DDN11], and the spectrum
                                           of the Laplace operator on graphs corresponding to self-similar groups [DGL23]. In all of these studies,
                                           the recursive nature of the graph sequences leads to an iterative scheme for the corresponding graph
                                           polynomials. By analyzing such an induced dynamical system, the authors in [BLR17, BLR20, CR21,
                                           RL19] successfully described the zero sets of the graph polynomials and as a consequence the (non-
                                           )analyticity of the limiting free energy.
                                              The zeros of graph polynomials are also essential in understanding the computational complexity of
                                           the evaluation of graph polynomials, see [PR17]. In particular, such zeros for sequences of recursive
                                           graphs played a crucial role behind the scenes in the results from [GGŠ17, PR19], which focus on the
                                           complexity of approximating the independence polynomial values for bounded degree graphs.
                                              This paper aims to establish a unified framework for describing graph polynomials on sequences of
                                           recursive graphs. We focus specifically on one type of graph polynomial – the independence polynomial,
                                           also known as the partition function of the hard-core model. We introduce quite general (recursive)
                                           algorithms for generating recursive graph sequences and analyze the resulting zero sets.
                                              Informally, our algorithm works in the following way. Starting with a graph G0 with k marked vertices
                                           labeled 1, . . . , k, we iteratively construct a sequence of graphs (Gn )n≥0 , each with k marked vertices.

1.2. Independence polynomial. In this paper, unless otherwise stated, by a graph G we always mean
a multi-graph, that is, G is given by a pair (V (G), E(G)), where V (G) is a finite vertex set and E(G) is a
finite edge multi-set of undordered pairs in V (G). As usual, the vertex degree of v ∈ V (G) is the number
of adjacent edges, that is, the number of pairs {v, w} ∈ E(G). We call any map σ : V (G) → {0, 1} a
vertex assignment on G. We say that such σ is independent if σ −1 (1) := σ −1 ({1}) ⊆ V (G) forms an
independent set in G, that is, if no two vertices in σ −1 (1) form an edge of G.
   The (univariant) independence polynomial of G is defined as

corresponds to the partition function of the hard-core model, a model from statistical physics commonly
used to model gasses.

nomials for large graphs. It turns out that the exact computation of partition functions is almost always

algorithms for the approximation of partition functions was shown by Patel and Regts [PR17].

been extensively studied for the set of all graphs whose vertex degrees are bounded by some uniform

in a neighborhood of the real interval between two critical parameters λ− < 0 < λ+ , which implies the

discuss the boundedness of zeros of the independence polynomial for different sequences of graphs con-
verging to cubic lattices. These results are closely related to what we prove here for recursive sequences
of graphs.

1.3. Recursive definition. Let us formally describe an abstract recursive procedure for producing se-
quences of graphs that we study in this paper. Each graph in such a sequence (Gn )n≥0 will have k ≥ 2
distinct marked vertices, labeled 1, . . . , k. A recursive step will consist of taking m ≥ 2 copies of the
previous graph and connecting some of the copied marked vertices according to a specific rule, while also
marking and labeling k vertices of the resulting graph. This construction will be governed by a gluing
data (H, Σ, Υ, Φ), where H will specify which marked vertices of the m copies are joined, the pair (Σ, Υ)
will specify how they are joined, and Φ will specify how the new marked vertices are assigned.

Definition 1.2 (Gluing data (H, Σ, Υ, Φ)). A (formal ) gluing data (with parameters m ≥ 2 and k ≥ 2)
is a quadruple (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) where each item is specified as follows:
     • H is a multi-hypergraph with the vertex set V (H) = {w1 , . . . , wm } and edge multi-set E(H).
       This means that the edges of H are given by non-empty subsets e ⊆ V (H), where a single subset
       may occur multiple times in E(H). We assume that each vertex of H is contained in exactly k
       edges, and that each of these edges e has a distinct label ℓ(e) ∈ {1, . . . , k}. In other words, for
       each j ∈ {1, . . . , k}, the edges of H labeled j specify a partition of V (H) = {w1 , . . . , wm }. We
       call the labeled multi-graph H the gluing scheme.
     • Σ = (Σe )e∈E(H) is a collection of (non-empty) connected multi-graphs, which we call connecting
       graphs. We assume that each Σe contains a “special” marked vertex ve ∈ V (Σe ), which we call
       the root of Σe .
     • Υ = (Υe )e∈E(H) is a collection of (not necessarily injective) maps Υe : e → V (Σe ), which we call
       attaching maps.
     • Finally, Φ : {1, . . . , k} → E(H) is an injective map, which we call the labeling map.
We call the pair (H, Φ) as above the reduced gluing data (with parameters m, k).

  We now specify the recursive step governed by formal gluing data.

Definition 1.3 (Graph recursion R). Let (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) be a gluing data with parameters m ≥ 2 and
k ≥ 2 as above, and suppose that we are given a graph G with k distinct marked vertices v1 , . . . , vk .
Here and below, we think of each vertex vj as having the label j. We now define a new marked graph
R(G) = R(H,Σ,Υ,Φ) (G) as follows:
    (I) First, we take m copies G(1), . . . , G(m) of G, where each copy G(i) is associated to the vertex wi
        of the gluing scheme H.
   (II) Then for each edge e = {wi1 , . . . , wis } ∈ E(H) with label ℓ(e) ∈ {1, . . . , k}, we connect together
        the marked vertices labeled ℓ(e) in the copies G(i1 ), . . . , G(is ) by identifying them with the
        vertices Υe (wi1 ), . . . , Υe (wis ) in Σe , respectively. We denote the obtained graph R(H,Σ,Υ) (G).
        We observe that, by construction, the edges of H containing only a single vertex do not lead to
        connections between vertices of different copies of G.

  (III) Finally, we assign k marked vertices in the graph R(H,Σ,Υ) (G) according to the labeling map Φ:
        the label j ∈ {1, . . . , k} is given to the vertex of R(H,Σ,Υ) (G) that corresponds to the root of Σe ,
        where e = Φ(j) ∈ E(H).
The resulting marked graph, which we denote R(G) = R(H,Σ,Υ,Φ) (G), is said to be obtained from G
according to the gluing data (H, Σ, Υ, Φ).
   In this way, given a gluing data (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) with parameters m, k and an arbitrary starting graph
G0 with k distinct marked vertices labeled 1, . . . , k, we produce a sequence (Gn )n≥0 of marked graphs
defined by Gn+1 := R(H,Σ,Υ,Φ) (G0 ), each with k distinct marked vertices labeled 1, . . . , k.
Remark 1.4. There are several natural canonical choices for the connecting graphs Σe , each depending
only on the cardinality s = #e. The first option are the s-pods K1,s – the complete (1, s)-bipartite
graphs. The s-pods are used when defining Cayley trees. The second option are the s-cycles, which are
used to construct Schreier graphs. Other natural options include the complete graphs Ks or simply the
singletons K1 . In the latter case, the corresponding marked vertices in the s copies of the original graph
are identified to a single vertex. This choice is, for example, used to define diamond hierarchical lattices
or other hierarchical graph systems. Figure 1 illustrates the impact of a specific choice of the graphs Σe .

Definition 1.5 (Dynamics on the label set). A reduced gluing data (H, Φ) with parameters m, k specifies
a dynamical system Λ = Λ(H,Φ) : {1, . . . , k} → {1, . . . , k} on the labels: namely, we send each label
j ∈ {1, . . . , k} to Λ(j) = ℓ(Φ(j)). A label j is called periodic if Λp (j) = j for some p ≥ 1. Clearly, every
label is eventually mapped to a periodic one by Λ.
   The number #Φ(j) is called the local degree of a label j and the number #Φ(j) − 1 is called the
multiplicity of j. We say that j is critical if #Φ(j) ≥ 2. Similarly to ramification portraits for rational
maps, we define the portrait of the reduced gluing data (H, Φ): it is a directed graph with the vertex
set {1, . . . , k}, where we put a directed edge from every j ∈ {1, . . . , k} to Λ(j) = ℓ(Φ(j)) and label it by
“#Φ(j) : 1” if #Φ(j) ≥ 2.
   We can now introduce the technical assumptions on the gluing data that are necessary for our main
result.
Definition 1.6 (Non-degenerate, stable, and expanding gluing data). Suppose (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) is a gluing
data with parameters m, k. We say that it is:
     • non-degenerate if the induced dynamical system Λ = Λ(H,Φ) has no periodic critical labels;
     • stable if it is non-degenerate and the connecting graph ΣΦ(j) for each periodic label j ∈ {1, . . . , k}
        is a singleton;
     • expanding if there exists n ≥ 1 such that the gluing data (Hn , Σn , Υn , Φn ) of the n-th iterate of
        the graph recursion R = R(H,Σ,Υ,Φ) satisfies the following condition: the edges Φn (j) and Φn (ℓ)
        form disjoint subsets of V (Hn ) for all distinct labels j, ℓ ∈ {1, . . . , k}.
Remark 1.7. Suppose (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) is an arbitrary gluing data with parameters m, k, and (H, Σ′ , Υ′ , Φ)
is the “simplified” gluing data where each graph Σe , e ∈ E(H), is replaced by a singleton graph Σ′e = K1 .
Let (Gn )n≥0 be a sequence of marked graphs defined by this “simplified” gluing data, starting with some
connected graph G0 . Then the following are true:
     (i) The gluing data (H, Σ, Υ, Φ) is non-degenerate if and only if the vertex degrees of Gn are uniformly
         bounded.

1.4. Maximally independent starting graphs. We now provide the final technical notion needed for
our main result.

    We close the introduction with another example.
Example 1.10. Let us define the Chebychev recursion as follows. Starting with a graph G0 with two
marked vertices labeled 1, 2, we define Gn+1 by first taking two copies of Gn , then identifying the vertices
with labels 2 in these copies, and afterward assigning the new labels 1 and 2 to the two vertices previously
labeled 1.
   If the starting graph G0 is K2 , then Gn is simply a path whose length grows exponentially as 2n .
None of these paths are maximally independent, and in fact, the zeros of the corresponding independence
polynomials, which all happen to lie on the negative real axis, are unbounded.
   On the other hand, if G0 is a tripod K1,3 , with marked vertices at two of the three leaves, then G1
is maximally independent, and our main result implies that the zeros of the independence polynomials
ZGn (λ) are uniformly bounded.
   The Chebychev recursion from above shows that boundedness of the zeros of the independence poly-
nomials ZGn (λ) for a recursive sequence of graphs (Gn )n≥0 may depend on the starting graph G0 . It also
shows that our main result is false when the assumption on G0 being maximally independent is removed.
