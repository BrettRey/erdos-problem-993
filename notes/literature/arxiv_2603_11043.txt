1    Introduction
The concentration function of a random variable X at value t > 0 will be
defined as Q(X, t) = supx∈R P(X ∈ (x, x + t)). We will denote the maximum
concentration at a point by Q(X) = supx∈R P(X = x). If X is an integer
random variable, then simply Q(X, 1) = Q(X) = supx∈Z P(X = x).
    For any random variable and a positive integer k we define Qk (X) =
supA:|A|=k P(X ∈ A), that is Qk (X) is the sum of k largest probability masses
of (the law of) X. If the law of X is µ, we will use the symbols X and
µ interchangeably, so Q(µ) is the same as Q(X) and similarly for other
functionals in this paper.
    Let α1 , . . . , αn ∈ (0, 1]. What is the maximum possible value of Q(Sn )
(or Q(Sn , L)) if Sn = X1 + · · · + Xn and X1 , . . . , Xn are independent random
variables such that for each i we have Q(Xi ) ≤ αi (or Q(Xi , λi ) ≤ αi ))?
These questions and their variants have been the subject of quite a lot of re-
search over the past 90 years. They originated in the works of Lévy, Doeblin
and Kolmogorov, also in those of Littlewood and Offord, and were further
studied by Esseen, Kesten, Rogozin and others, see, for example, the paper
[24] and the references therein. Nevertheless, for a general sequence of con-
straints, inequalities obtained using classical characteristic function methods,
e.g. [24, 30] can be suboptimal by at least a constant factor.
    A subtler type of results are related to determining sequences of ran-
dom variables that satisfy the same conditions as (Xi ) and maximize the
concentration function value of the sum. If the extremizing sequence has a
simple structure, we can easily get sharp quantitative bounds. For the inter-
val version (in the case when λ1 = . . . λn = L) optimal results of this kind
have been obtained in [12] (single-dimensional case) and [15] (asymptotically,
high-dimensional case). The question of optimal bounds for the pointwise
version of the problem has remained open. While the two versions of the
concentration function problem look similar, they are not equivalent and
the extremizers are different in general: they are symmetric in the interval
case, see [12, 15], and non-symmetric in the pointwise case, see [14] and the
definition of να below. This makes the pointwise problem more technically
challenging and we need to use completely different ideas in this work.
    It is known, see Ushakov [30], that the general problem of maximizing the
pointwise concentration Q(Sn ) given pointwise bounds for the summands can
be reduced to the case of integer random variables, see Corollary 1.2 below.
To our knowledge, the best previous results for the concentration function

                                       2
problem for integer random variables, or its equivalent formulation in the
language of sums, have been obtained in [11, 19, 21]. For example, building
on Gabriel’s inequality, see Hardy, Littlewood and Pólya [11], Lev [19] showed
that if each Xi is a uniform random variable on a set of size ki then Q(Sn ) ≤
Q(Y1 + · · · + Yn ) where Y1 , . . . , Yn are independent and Yi is distributed
uniformly on the interval {0, . . . , ki − 1}. In other words, in the uniform
case, removing “gaps” in the supports of all the random summands can only
increase the maximum probability of the sum. Madiman, Wang and Woo [21]
obtained a similar result that works for a more general family of distributions
of integers that includes all two-point distributions, see Theorem 2.27 below.

2.1    Definitions
A measure µ on integers is called unimodal if there is m ∈ Z such that
µ({m}) ≥ µ({m + 1}) ≥ . . . and µ({m}) ≥ µ({m − 1}) ≥ . . . . Any such m
is called a mode of µ.
     In their work on rearrangement inequalities, Hardy, Littlewood and Pólya
[11] introduced the notation µ+ , + µ and µ∗ which will be defined as fol-
lows. Let µ be a finite measure on integers. We call a measure µ′ on
Z a rearrangement of µ if there exists a bijection f : Z → Z such that
µ′ ({x}) = µ({f (x)}) for each x ∈ Z. µ+ is the rearrangement of µ that
satisfies µ+ ({0}) ≥ µ+ ({1}) ≥ µ+ ({−1}) ≥ µ+ ({2}) ≥ µ+ ({−2}) ≥ . . . .
Similarly + µ is the rearrangement of µ that satisfies + µ({0}) ≥ + µ({−1}) ≥

µ has a symmetric decreasing rearrangement and denote this symmetric
measure by µ∗ . Note that for a symmetric unimodal measure on integers

Lemma 2.11. t(α1 , . . . , αn ) is non-decreasing in each of αi ∈ (0, 1].

Proof We omit the details as the proof, based on Krein-Milman theorem,
see, e.g., proof of Theorem 1 in [14] or Theorem 1.5 of [20], is standard: for
any sequence of measures µ1 , . . . , µn with Q(µi ) = αi , i ∈ {1, . . . , n} we can
represent the ith measure as a mixture of extremal measures for αi′ (which
are extreme points of the convex set of measures with concentration function
at most αi′ ), where αi′ is any number such that 1 ≥ αi′ ≥ αi . Then taking
the expectation over all random variables except the i-th one we pick the
component of the mixture that maximizes the concentration function, and
doing this for each i obtain an sequence µ′1 , . . . , µ′n of (extremal) measures
with Q(µ′i ) = αi′ for all i and Q(µ′1 ∗ · · · ∗ µn ) ≥ Q(µ1 ∗ · · · ∗ µn ).        □
   The proof of monotonicity of the optimal value for standard extremal
sequences is also simple, but with a different argument.
