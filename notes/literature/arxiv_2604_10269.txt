                                               CONTRACTIBLE INDEPENDENCE COMPLEXES OF TREES

                                                                       MY HANH PHAM AND THANH VU




arXiv:2604.10269v2 [math.CO] 14 Apr 2026
                                                  Abstract. We show that the independence complex of a tree is contractible if
                                                  and only if it can be reduced to a path Pn with n ≡ 1 (mod 3) by a sequence of
                                                  truncation moves at branching points. As a consequence of our method, we also
                                                  characterize the trees for which the independence polynomial evaluated at −1 is
                                                  equal to 1 or −1.




                                                                               1. Introduction
                                              Let G be a finite simple graph. An independent set in G is a set of pairwise
                                           nonadjacent vertices. The maximum cardinality of an independent set in G is called
                                           the independence number of G, denoted by α(G). The independence polynomial of G
                                           is defined by
                                                                     I(G; x) = s0 + s1 x + · · · + sα(G) xα(G) ,
                                           where si is the number of independent sets of size i in G.
                                              The independence polynomial of a graph was first studied by Gutman and Harary
                                           [8], and has since been investigated by many others; see [2, 7, 9, 10] and the references
                                           therein. For a survey, we refer to [14]. There are only a few classes of graphs for which
                                           the independence polynomial is computed explicitly [11, 16]. For the independence
                                           polynomial of trees, one central open problem is unimodality. This property is known
                                           to hold for the class of claw-free graphs [3].
                                              The independence complex of G is the simplicial complex whose faces are the in-
                                           dependent sets of G. Consequently, the independence polynomial of a graph is the
                                           f -polynomial of its independence complex. By results of Kozlov [13] and Ehrenborg
                                           and Hetyei [4], the independence complex of a tree is either contractible or homotopy
                                           equivalent to a sphere. In particular, I(G; −1) ∈ {−1, 0, 1} for any tree G, and the in-
                                           dependence complex of G is contractible if and only if I(G; −1) = 0. Kim [12] recently
                                           proved that the independence complex of a graph with no induced cycle of length di-
                                           visible by 3 is either contractible or homotopy equivalent to a sphere. Ehrenborg
                                           and Hetyei also determined the homotopy type of the independence complex of path
                                           graphs. In particular, Ind(Pn ), the independence complex of a path on n vertices, is
                                           contractible if and only if n ≡ 1 (mod 3). Engström [5] characterized trees for which
                                           the independence complex is contractible. This was later generalized by Adamaszek
                                           [1] and Faridi and Holleben [6]. In this paper, we completely characterize trees for

   We now introduce some terminology to make the previous statement more precise.
Let u be a vertex of G. The neighborhood of u in G, denoted by NG (u), is the set of
all vertices v such that {u, v} is an edge in G. The degree of u is the cardinality of
NG (u). A vertex u of G is called a branching point if degG (u) > 2, and it is called a
leaf if degG (u) = 1.
   Let G be a simple graph and u be a branching point of G. A path starting at u
and ending at a leaf is called a pure branch if every vertex on the path, except for u,
has degree at most 2 in G. We define the type of a branch P to be the residue of its
length modulo 3. We define the following moves at branching points:
   (1) If there exist a branch of type 1 and a branch of type 2, remove all vertices of
       G except those belonging to these two branches.
   (2) If there is more than one pure branch of the same type, remove all but one
       such branch.
   (3) If P is a pure branch of G at u of type 0, remove all vertices on this branch
       except u.
Definition 1.1. Let G be a simple graph. Let H be a graph obtained by one of the
moves above. Then H is said to be a reduction of G, and we say that G is reducible
to H.
  The characterization of trees with a contractible independence complex is as follows.
Theorem 1.2. Let G be a tree. Then Ind(G) is contractible if and only if G is
reducible to a path Pn with n ≡ 1 (mod 3).
  To characterize graphs with I(G; −1) = 1 or −1, we need to take parity into
account; hence, we introduce an additional definition.
Definition 1.3. Let G be a simple graph and let u be a branching point of G. Assume
that u has t ≥ 2 pure branches of the same type, and let M be the move obtained
by removing t − 1 of these branches. We say that the move M is odd if there is an
odd number of branches whose length has residue 4 (mod 6) when the type is 1, and
residue 2 (mod 6) when the type is 2. Otherwise, M is called an even move.
  Let M be the move of removing t pure branches of type 0 at u. Then M is called
an odd move if an odd number of these branches have length congruent to 3 (mod 6);
otherwise, it is called an even move.
Theorem 1.4. Let G be a tree. Then the following statements hold:
   (1) I(G; −1) = 1 if and only if G is reducible to Pn with n ≡ 5, 0 (mod 6) by an
       even number of odd moves, or to Pn with n ≡ 2, 3 (mod 6) by an odd number
       of odd moves;

        odd number of odd moves, or to Pn with n ≡ 2, 3 (mod 6) by an even number
        of odd moves.

                    2. Independence polynomial of trees at −1
  Let G = (V (G), E(G)) be a simple graph with vertex set V (G) and edge set E(G).
Let A ⊆ V (G). We denote by G − A the induced subgraph of G on V (G) \ A. When
A = {u}, we write G − u instead of G − {u}. The closed neighborhood of u in G is
NG [u] = NG (u) ∪ {u}.
  We have the following standard lemma [8].
Lemma 2.1. The following assertions hold:
  (1) If G1 and G2 are disjoint graphs, then I(G1 ∪ G2 ; x) = I(G1 ; x) I(G2 ; x).
  (2) For every v ∈ V (G), we have I(G; x) = I(G − v; x) + x I(G − NG [v]; x).
  The independence polynomial of paths is well known [15]. In particular,

Lemma 2.3. Let G be a simple graph and let u be a branching point of G. Assume
that u has a pure branch of type 1 and a pure branch of type 2. Then I(G; −1) = 0.
Proof. Let P = u, v1 , . . . , v3k+1 and Q = u, w1 , . . . , w3ℓ+2 be pure branches at u of type
1 and type 2, respectively, where k, ℓ are nonnegative integers. By assumption, G − u
has a connected component consisting of v1 , . . . , v3k+1 , which is isomorphic to P3k+1 .
Moreover, G−NG [u] has a connected component {w2 , . . . , w3ℓ+2 }, which is isomorphic
to P3ℓ+1 . By Lemma 2.1 and Lemma 2.2, I(G − u; −1) = 0 and I(G − NG [u]; −1) = 0.
Applying Lemma 2.1, we conclude that I(G; −1) = 0.                                            □
Lemma 2.4. Let G be a simple graph and let u be a branching point of G. Assume
that u has t ≥ 2 pure branches of type 1. Let H be the graph obtained by removing all
but one such branch. Assume that among the removed branches, there are s branches
whose length has residue 4 (mod 6). Then
                                   I(G; −1) = (−1)s I(H; −1).
Proof. For j = 1, . . . , t, let the j-th pure branch from u of type 1 be u, vj,1 , . . . , vj,3kj +1 .
Let
                     H = G \ {vj,ℓ | j = 2, . . . , t, ℓ = 1, . . . , 3kj + 1}
be the graph obtained by removing t − 1 pure branches of type 1 from G.
  Both G − u and H − u contain a connected component isomorphic to the path
P3k1 +1 ; hence I(G − u; −1) = I(H − u; −1) = 0.

  We now show that any tree can be reduced to Pn for some n.
Lemma 2.7. Let G be a tree. Then G is reducible to Pn for some n ≥ 1.
Proof. We proceed by induction on n, the number of vertices of G. The base case
n ≤ 2 is trivial.
   Note that if G has no branching points, then it is isomorphic to Pn . Hence, it
suffices to show that if G has a branching point, then it contains a branching point
with at least two pure branches. Let v be a leaf of G. Consider a path from v to
another leaf of G, and let w be the branching point closest to v, so that the branch
from w to v is pure. Let H be the graph obtained by removing this branch. If H
is a path, then w is a branching point with at least two branches. Otherwise, by
induction, H has a branching point with at least two pure branches.
   If this branching point is w itself, then we are done. Otherwise, let this branching
point of H be w1 . If the pure branches of H at w1 do not contain w, then they are also
pure branches of G. Hence, we are done if w1 has two pure branches not containing
w. If w1 has a pure branch containing w, note that w cannot be a leaf of G, so this
pure branch must extend beyond w. This extended path from w is a pure branch of
G at w. Hence, w has at least two pure branches. The conclusion follows.             □
