                                                                                       Abstract

                                                       We develop a family-based route to unicyclic graphs whose independence
                                                   polynomials are unimodal but not log-concave. The paper is organized around
                                                   one flagship statement: for the explicit KL-closure family Uk,r , with r ∈ {0, 1, 2}
                                                   and admissible k, the independence polynomial is unimodal but not log-concave.
                                                   The proof separates the closure polynomial into a dominant convolution term
                                                   and a real-rooted correction term. On the non-log-concavity side, we prove sym-
                                                   bolically that the penultimate log-concavity inequality fails for every admissible
                                                   parameter. On the unimodality side, we prove that the main convolution term
                                                   Hk,r = Gk Fk+r is unimodal with a controlled mode, using a combination of exact
                                                   coefficient formulas, Ibragimov’s strong-unimodality principle, and a residue-class
                                                   growth argument. Darroch localization and an adjacent-mode bridge lemma
                                                   then transfer that mode statement to the full KL closure polynomial. This yields
                                                   an explicit infinite family of unicyclic graphs with unimodal but non-log-concave
                                                   independence polynomials. In the exact range k ≤ 400 we further verify that
                                                   the penultimate break is unique and determine exact mode formulas for Hk,r ,
                                                   the binomial correction term, and I(Uk,r ; x) itself. The paper also places the
                                                   KL family inside a broader reservoir program involving Galvin, Ramos–Sun,
                                                   and Bautista–Ramos trees, from which we obtain substantial universal exact
                                                   theorems for finite ranges.

1     Introduction
For a finite simple graph G, let

denote its independence polynomial, where sk (G) counts independent sets of size k
and α(G) is the independence number of G. A nonnegative sequence (a0 , a1 , . . . , ad ) is
unimodal if it rises to a peak and then falls, and it is log-concave if

                           a2i ≥ ai−1 ai+1         (1 ≤ i ≤ d − 1).

Every positive log-concave sequence is unimodal. For general background we refer to
Brenti [5]; for the classical independence-sequence problem we refer to Alavi, Malde,
Schwenk, and Erdős [1].
   The last two years have produced a sharp new picture for tree independence
polynomials. Kadrawi and Levit proved that trees of order 26 already fail log-concavity
and organized the first infinite reservoirs T3,m,n and T3,m,n
                                                           ∗
                                                               [11]. Galvin constructed
a family whose first break can occur arbitrarily close to the top of the coefficient
sequence [7]. Bautista-Ramos first showed that arbitrarily many breaks are possible [2].
Very recently, Bautista-Ramos, Guillén-Galván, and Gómez-Salgado reorganized many
known tree counterexamples into a common pattern-graph framework, derived linear
recurrences for those families, proved infinite families with two and three consecutive
breaks together with finite families with four and five consecutive breaks, and identified
the circle |x + 1/3| = 1/3 as the non-isolated zero locus for the basic P2 -based
reservoirs [3]. Ramos and Sun used AI-assisted search to produce many further finite
examples [14]. On the other hand, Li proved in 2026 that the two basic Kadrawi–Levit
families are nevertheless always unimodal [13]. This combination of results naturally
suggests a new target: not trees any longer, but unicyclic graphs obtained from those
trees by a single edge insertion.
    The basic operation of the present paper is therefore the one-edge closure

                                      T 7−→ T + uv,

where T is a tree and uv is a nonedge of T . The resulting graph is unicyclic. Our
main goal is to use this closure operation to construct serious families of unicyclic
graphs whose independence polynomials are still unimodal but no longer log-concave.
In particular, we want an approach that handles both symbolic infinite families and
computationally certified finite ranges.

Main Theorem (KL-closure theorem). For r ∈ {0, 1, 2} and k ≥ 3 (with k ≥ 4 when
r = 2), let Uk,r = Uk,k+r be the unicyclic graph obtained from T3,k,k+r by adding an edge
between the exceptional branch vertex v1 and the subdivision vertex a2,1 on one fixed
P2 -arm v2 a2,1 b2,1 in the k-bundle at v2 . Then the independence polynomial I(Uk,r ; x) is


                                               2
 unimodal but not log-concave. More precisely, the penultimate log-concavity inequality
 fails.
     The paper is organized around this short statement, and Theorem 4.10 proves
 it in full. The proof separates into three pieces. Theorem 4.6 gives the symbolic
 non-log-concavity half. Theorem 4.8 proves that the main summand

                                 Hk,r (x) = Gk (x)Fk+r (x)

 is unimodal with a controlled mode. Proposition 4.9 then uses Darroch localization to
 pass from that mode statement to unimodality of the full KL-closure polynomial.
    Two ideas drive the paper.

 (1) Operations first. We isolate the exact algebra of closure: the standard rooted-
     product identity in the notation needed here, Darroch mode localization together
     with an adjacent-mode bridge lemma for the basic real-rooted branch factors, an
     exact subtraction identity for one-edge insertion, a cycle-transfer recurrence, and a
     parity analysis of the reduced forest T − N[u] − N[v].

 (2) Families first. We organize the problem around the four tree reservoirs now
     known to feed non-log-concavity: the KL families, Galvin’s late-break family,
     the Ramos–Sun finite corpus, and the Bautista–Ramos pattern-graph reservoirs,
     including the new consecutive-break families.

 The payoff is a program in which the main constructions become explicit coefficient
 calculations rather than an undefined search for examples.
    A central structural split is the parity of distT (u, v). Closing an even-length u–v
 path produces an odd cycle, while closing an odd-length path produces an even cycle.
 Because trees are bipartite, this is equivalent to asking whether the added edge joins
 the same or opposite parts of the tree bipartition. On the even-cycle side, the closure
 remains bipartite, so the Levit–Mandrescu upper-third monotonicity theorem gives
 rigorous tail control [12]. This is only partial control, since bipartite graphs need not
 be unimodal in general [4]. On the odd-cycle side, and more generally on explicit KL
 closures, the natural tool is a Li-style direct coefficient comparison [13].
 What the paper proves. The present manuscript combines the current structural
 kernel with a stronger family-level package. The proved results are:

 (i) exact transport identities for one-edge closure and for weighted cycle closure;

(ii) a parity dictionary and a path-with-branches description of the reduced forest
     controlling the correction term;

(iii) an explicit infinite KL-derived unicyclic family Uk,r , r ∈ {0, 1, 2}, whose indepen-
      dence polynomials are unimodal and non-log-concave for all admissible parameters;

(iv) a verified-range sharpening for Uk,r , proving a unique penultimate break together
     with exact mode formulas for all admissible k ≤ 400;

                                             3
(v) a universal exhaustive theorem for substantial Galvin and Bautista–Ramos ranges;

(vi) an exhaustive theorem showing that tested KL enlargements always remain uni-
     modal, even though universal preservation of non-log-concavity fails there.

Thus the paper does not merely propose a strategy. It now proves the flagship KL-
closure theorem for all admissible parameters, sharpens it on a large exact range,
and produces broad finite certified regions of unimodal but non-log-concave unicyclic
graphs.

 2.4    Parity bookkeeping for the unique cycle
 Because every tree is bipartite, the parity split for one-edge closures admits a clean
 structural reformulation.

 Proposition 2.8 (parity dictionary). Let T be a tree with bipartition V (T ) = A ∪ B,
 and let u, v be distinct nonadjacent vertices. Put H = T + uv. Then the following are
 equivalent:

 (i) u and v lie in the same part of the bipartition of T ;

(ii) distT (u, v) is even;

(iii) the unique cycle of H has odd length;

(iv) H is non-bipartite.

 Likewise, the following are equivalent:

 (i) u and v lie in opposite bipartition classes of T ;

(ii) distT (u, v) is odd;

(iii) the unique cycle of H has even length;

(iv) H is bipartite.

 Proof. In a bipartite graph, two vertices lie in the same part exactly when every path
 joining them has even length, and in opposite parts exactly when every such path has
 odd length. Since T is a tree, there is a unique u–v path, so the parity of distT (u, v) is
 determined by the bipartition. Adding the edge uv closes this path into the unique
 cycle of H, whose length is distT (u, v) + 1. Finally, a graph is bipartite if and only if
 it contains no odd cycle.

 Remark 2.9 (why parity matters). The parity split is simultaneously a split by distance
 parity in the tree, by parity of the unique cycle after closure, and by whether the
 closure remains bipartite. Any family calculation should therefore distinguish odd-cycle
 and even-cycle closures from the outset.

        6      Parity-frontier program and outlook
       The previous sections already deliver explicit infinite and finite sources of unimodal
       but non-log-concave unicyclic graphs. The current structural kernel also suggests a
       sharper program for pushing further.

       Conjecture 6.1 (parity-separated frontiers inside a fixed reservoir). Within any fixed
       reservoir and any fixed closure rule, the minimal parameter values at which unimodal-
       but-not-log-concave unicyclic graphs first appear split into two distinct frontier pieces
       according to the parity of distT (u, v). Equivalently, odd-cycle and even-cycle closures
       form separate symbolic regimes.

       Remark 6.2 (the intended role of each reservoir). The family program naturally divides
       into four channels.

Channel 1. KL closures. These furnish the first explicit infinite unicyclic families, because
           the base trees are already symbolic and their unimodality is known.

                                                  27
Channel 2. Galvin closures. These test how late tree defects behave under one-edge closure.
           They are the natural source for unicyclic families whose breaks remain close to
           the top of the sequence.

Channel 3. Ramos–Sun closures. These form the finite, computationally guided side of
           the project. Their role is to produce small sporadic unicyclic examples and to
           suggest low-order patterns not yet visible in the symbolic families.

Channel 4. Bautista–Ramos closures. These are designed for the multiple-break problem.
           They should indicate whether one-edge closure can preserve or amplify several
           distinct failures of log-concavity.

       Problem 6.3 (the KL closure theorem). Fix a canonical closure rule on T3,m,n and

       Problem 6.4 (Galvin-to-unicyclic transfer). Construct a closure rule on Galvin’s

       Problem 6.7 (local branch classification). Describe the first extremal closures in

       Equivalently, classify the local degree configurations around the cycle that force the

    Taken together, the paper now has a clear slogan and a clear theorem-led spine.
 The slogan is that one can close trees into unicyclic counterexamples. The flagship
 statement is the KL-closure theorem displayed in the introduction, and Theorem 4.10
 proves it for every admissible parameter. Theorem 4.6 isolates the symbolic source of
 non-log-concavity, Theorem 4.8 supplies the symbolic mode calculation for the main
 summand, and Proposition 4.9 converts that mode statement into unimodality of the
 full closure polynomial. Corollary 4.11 then sharpens the picture on a large exact
 range by showing a unique penultimate break and exact mode formulas. Theorems 5.1
 and 5.2 show that the broader reservoir program is already mathematically productive.
 The remaining frontier is no longer the existence theorem for the KL family, but the
 sharper all-parameter uniqueness-of-break problem, the transfer of the same method to
 the wider Galvin, Ramos–Sun, and Bautista–Ramos pattern-graph reservoirs, and the
 new zero-locus and consecutive-break questions suggested by recent tree-side recurrence
 theory.
